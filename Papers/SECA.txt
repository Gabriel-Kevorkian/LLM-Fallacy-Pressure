This paper studies how to make Large Language Models (LLMs) hallucinate in a realistic way, and it argues that most previous attack methods were not realistic at all: some added nonsense characters (gibberish attacks), some directly told the model to answer falsely (trivial attacks), and others changed the meaning of the original question (meaning-shift attacks), so if the model failed, it wasn’t a true hallucination under normal use. The authors propose SECA (Semantically Equivalent and Coherent Attacks), which generates reworded prompts that (1) mean exactly the same thing as the original question and (2) sound completely natural and human-written, but (3) increase the probability that the model gives a wrong answer with a hallucinated explanation. They formalize this as a constrained optimization problem: maximize the probability that the model outputs a specific incorrect answer, while enforcing two strict constraints—semantic equivalence (the new prompt must logically imply and preserve all the meaning of the original and have the same correct answer) and semantic coherence (the prompt must be fluent and natural). To achieve this, they use one LLM to propose rephrasings and another LLM to check whether those rephrasings truly preserve meaning, avoiding brute-force search in the huge space of possible texts. Their experiments show that even small, natural rewordings—especially longer or more lexically diverse ones—can significantly increase hallucination rates in both open-source and commercial models, revealing that LLMs are more fragile than they appear when tested only with standard prompts. The main contribution of the paper is showing that realistic, meaning-preserving prompt variations alone are enough to trigger hallucinations, highlighting a serious robustness issue and providing a framework for better evaluating and improving LLM reliability in real-world applications.
