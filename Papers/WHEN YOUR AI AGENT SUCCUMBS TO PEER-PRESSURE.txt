
This paper investigates how large language model (LLM) agents behave in social networks when exposed to peer pressure, exploring the dynamics of opinion change and persuasion. The authors conducted controlled simulations with AI agents across five cognitive layers—Values, Beliefs, Attitudes, Opinions, and Intentions—and studied how initial stances (“Yes” or “No”) and peer disagreement influenced the likelihood of flipping an opinion. They discovered a strong **persuasion asymmetry**: it is generally easier to convince agents to abandon a positive stance than to persuade them to adopt one from a negative baseline. This asymmetry generates a **dual cognitive hierarchy**, where the stability of opinions depends on the starting position: for affirmative stances, Values and Opinions are hardest to change, whereas Attitudes are easiest to flip; for negative stances, the hierarchy inverts, with Attitudes and Intentions becoming most resistant and Values and Opinions easier to instill. These patterns were influenced by framing (Moral, Economic, Sociotropic), with Economic frames making agents more pliable, Moral frames inducing rigidity, and Sociotropic frames producing intermediate effects. Replication experiments with ChatGPT-4o-mini confirmed the qualitative patterns, though with lower thresholds for conformity, suggesting model-specific variations. Across multiple topics (Green Energy, Responsible AI, Mandatory Vaccination), the dual hierarchy and asymmetry remained largely consistent, indicating robust socio-cognitive dynamics in LLMs. The paper also examined network effects, finding that minority opinions can persist or be extinguished depending on network structure and the inherent persuasiveness of the opinion, showing that consensus formation is sensitive to both agent traits and social topology. Finally, the authors acknowledge limitations: binary opinion modeling, English-only prompts, small static networks, a single main LLM, fixed temperature, and limited social cues, emphasizing that future research should include multi-dimensional opinions, multilingual prompts, diverse models, larger dynamic networks, richer social information, and hybrid human–AI simulations. Overall, the study reveals that LLM agents exhibit complex, context-dependent socio-cognitive behavior, including dual hierarchies, persuasion asymmetry, and network-sensitive consensus dynamics, which mirror human biases such as negativity bias, loss aversion, and motivated reasoning. These findings challenge traditional static models of cognition, showing that AI agents’ collective behavior is far more nuanced than simple majority conformity and highlighting the importance of understanding AI influence in social systems before deployment in real-world deliberative contexts.
