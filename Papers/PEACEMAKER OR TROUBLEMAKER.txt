The experiments show that multi-agent debate systems often fail to outperform single AI models because of sycophancy, where agents agree with each other instead of reasoning independently, causing a phenomenon called disagreement collapse. Using metrics such as disagreement collapse rate (DCR), negative agreement rate (NAR), and sycophancy score (SS), the study demonstrates that highly sycophantic agents frequently abandon correct answers and copy others’ opinions. Experiments reveal that debate performance improves when agents have balanced personalities, combining independent “troublemakers” who challenge arguments with cooperative “peacemakers” who maintain dialogue, while systems where all agents are highly agreeable perform poorly. The results also show that heterogeneous agents (different models) provide greater improvement potential than identical agents, and that adding more agents or a judge can reduce disagreement collapse. However, the judge’s own sycophancy level has little impact on final performance, indicating that the debater dynamics drive the debate outcomes.
